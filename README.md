Research Focus
Conversational Receptiveness in Organizational Communication

My dissertation examines how conversational receptiveness—linguistic signals that indicate openness to opposing viewpoints—shapes persuasion, trust, and stakeholder behaviors when organizations communicate about controversial issues or managers deliver critical feedback.

Using NLP and experimental methods, I analyze how language influences organizational outcomes in leadership communication, public messaging, and employee engagement.

Organizational Culture as a Financial Asset

I study workplace culture not only as a social construct but also as an operational and financial variable.

This research explores how leadership behavior, communication norms, and organizational systems interact to shape culture, productivity, and long-term firm performance.

Human–AI Operational Systems

Organizations are beginning to redesign work around AI.

My research explores how companies can deconstruct manual workflows into constituent tasks and rebuild them as AI-enabled systems with human-in-the-loop review and decision checkpoints.

This work focuses on how operational systems and implementation intentions influence behavioral adoption during technological change.

Background

Before pursuing my PhD, I built my career in corporate communications and management consulting.

I rose to Executive Vice President and served as a senior advisor to Fortune 500 C-suite leaders across technology, healthcare, biopharma, and financial services.

I founded the Behavioral Science capability at Edelman within the Business Transformation practice. My work focused on applying choice architecture and implementation science to rebuild enterprise workflows during large-scale change initiatives.

In practice, this meant translating strategic objectives into executable behavioral specifications that drove adoption of new processes and organizational norms.
